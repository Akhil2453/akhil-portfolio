A maker mindset is not defined by access to expensive tools. It begins with a willingness to observe, question, build, test and improve. This distinction matters when educators are introduced to artificial intelligence. Without a clear learning purpose, AI can become another presentation tool. With a maker mindset, it becomes a partner in inquiry, rapid prototyping and reflective teaching practice.

## Start with a problem, not a platform

A productive faculty-development session should not begin with a catalogue of AI tools. It should begin with a teaching problem. A teacher may need to explain a difficult concept, create a differentiated activity, design an assessment or help learners connect theory with a real situation.

Once the problem is visible, AI can support the work. It can generate initial alternatives, simplify language, propose cases, create question banks or help organise a lesson. The educator still makes the academic decisions.

## Why making changes the conversation

When participants build something—even a paper prototype—the workshop becomes less abstract. Design decisions become visible. Assumptions are exposed. Feedback can be specific. The cycle of empathise, define, ideate, prototype and test stops being a diagram and becomes a lived process.

The same logic applies to AI. A weak prompt can be treated as an early prototype. Participants examine the output, identify what is missing and improve the prompt. This reduces the fear of making mistakes and encourages deliberate experimentation.

## Reflection is part of the build

An activity is not complete when the object, prompt or lesson plan is produced. Participants should be asked:

- What worked as expected?
- What did not work?
- Which assumption changed?
- What evidence influenced the revision?
- How could this approach be adapted for learners?

These questions convert an engaging workshop into professional learning.

## The educator remains accountable

AI-generated material must be checked for accuracy, bias, appropriateness and alignment with learning outcomes. The responsible position is neither rejection nor uncritical adoption. It is informed use, with the educator retaining academic judgement.

The most useful outcome of a maker-and-AI workshop is therefore not mastery of a particular tool. It is a repeatable way of working: begin with a meaningful problem, create a first version, test it, examine evidence and improve it.
