Activity-based teaching does not automatically produce activity-based assessment. A classroom may include models, discussions, simulations and prototypes, yet the final evaluation may still reward only a written description. This creates a gap between how learners work and how their learning is judged.

## Begin with observable evidence

The first design question is not, “How many marks should the activity carry?” It is, “What evidence would demonstrate the intended learning?”

For a design task, evidence may include:

- The quality of problem framing
- The relevance of observations or data
- The reasoning behind a selected concept
- The quality of prototyping and testing
- The learner’s response to feedback
- The clarity of technical communication

Once the evidence is defined, the activity and rubric can be aligned.

## Assess both process and outcome

A final prototype can fail for reasons that do not reflect weak learning. Equally, an attractive prototype may hide poor reasoning. A balanced assessment therefore examines both what was produced and how the learner arrived there.

A practical rubric can use four dimensions:

1. **Understanding:** Is the problem or requirement correctly interpreted?
2. **Application:** Are concepts and methods used appropriately?
3. **Iteration:** Is feedback or test evidence used to improve the work?
4. **Communication:** Can the learner explain decisions, limitations and next steps?

## Build checkpoints into the activity

Assessment should not occur only at the end. Short checkpoints allow the teacher to observe progress and give timely feedback. These can include a one-minute design rationale, a test log, a peer-review record or a brief demonstration.

## Avoid rewarding activity without learning

Attendance, enthusiasm and completion may be recorded, but they should not replace evidence of learning. The rubric must distinguish between being busy and making informed decisions.

When assessment is designed well, the activity becomes academically rigorous without losing its practical character. Learners understand what quality looks like, teachers gather richer evidence, and the classroom moves closer to authentic engineering practice.
